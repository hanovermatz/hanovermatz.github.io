Evolutionary Immunology

I am an immunologist studying the fundamental principles of adaptive immunity. My work synthesizes molecular biology, immunology, and comparative evolutionary approaches to better understand the human immune system.

The adaptive immune system is the product of millions of years of evolution in jawed vertebrates. Through the coordination of B cells, T cells, and antigen-presenting cells, the adaptive immune system generates antigen-specific responses and durable immunological memory. However, many mechanisms of the human immune system are still not fully understood, including the germinal center response which produces protective antibodies and the effector cells of humoral immunological memory. Investigating the dynamics of the immune system in different vertebrate species informs the fundamental principles governing immune protection. These principles can then be optimized in the context of vaccine design, cancer, and autoimmunity to treat human diseases.

Research Highlights

Mechanisms of B cell biology

B cells produce antibodies that defend the host from infectious pathogens. Through the germinal center response, B cells are able to undergo mutation, selection, and affinity maturation to improve their antibodies. By studying how this process functions, we determine how to improve the design of monoclonal antibody therapeutics and vaccines to stimulate immunity.

Evolution of B cell selection

Many vertebrate models can be studied to understand the evolution of the immune system. Sharks are the oldest living organisms with an immune system like humans: B cells, T cell, and immunoglobulins. My research has demonstrated that the fundamental principles of human germinal centers are present in the B cell selection sites of sharks, suggesting these are important conserved mechanisms throughout evolution.

Vaccine design and combating disease

What we learn from human, mouse, and comparative immunology studies informs how we can modulate vaccines to better induce protective and durable immunity. As we improve vaccine design, we can find new methods of combating infectious diseases.
